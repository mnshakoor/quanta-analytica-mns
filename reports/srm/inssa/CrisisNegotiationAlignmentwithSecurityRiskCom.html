Crisis Negotiation Alignment with Security Risk Competencies

Prepared by M. Nuri Shakoor, SRMP-C | ARAC International Inc. | Lladner Business Solutions, LLC

Source: Voss, C., & Raz, T. (2016). Never split the difference: Negotiating as if your life depended on it. HarperCollins.

The concepts developed by Chris Voss in Never Split the Difference, rooted in crisis negotiation, demonstrate strong alignment with the INSSA Core Competencies for certified Security Risk Management Professionals (SRMP) in the areas of Communication (C3), Stakeholder Engagement (D2), and Incident Management (D3).

Voss’s entire methodology centers on negotiation as an act of psychological investigation and deliberate influence, making the resulting actions highly relevant to these specific competencies, which require nuanced interaction and effective crisis handling.

Alignment with C3: Communication

The INSSA Competency Area C is "Leadership, Management and Implementation," under which C3 is Communication. Voss’s techniques fundamentally redefine negotiation as communication designed for results.

Voss Concept

Alignment with C3 (Communication)

Tactical Empathy and Active Listening

Effective communication starts with deep listening. Voss defines Tactical Empathy as recognizing the counterpart's perspective and vocalizing that recognition. This immediately conveys that the negotiator is listening, ensuring that communication pathways remain open and non-defensive. Tools like Mirroring (repeating critical words) and Labeling (acknowledging emotions aloud) are effortless yet highly effective communication tactics that build rapport and extract information.

Voice Tones

Voss stresses that successful communication depends heavily on how words are delivered. Utilizing the Late-Night FM DJ Voice (deep, slow, downward-inflecting) conveys control and authority without triggering defensiveness. The Positive/Playful Voice is the default, ensuring that the conversational dynamic is light, encouraging, and conducive to collaboration and mental agility.

Achieving Shared Understanding

Voss emphasizes that conversational niceties like "Yes" are not a substitute for real understanding. The true breakthrough in communication is achieving "That's right", which signifies the counterpart feels the negotiator genuinely grasps their situation. This is achieved through a Summary (paraphrasing + labeling), which ensures both parties are "on the same page".

Alignment with D2: Stakeholder Engagement

Competency D2, Stakeholder Engagement, falls under "Critical Incident Response" and requires professionals to manage relationships with external parties in often complex or high-stakes contexts. Voss’s system is designed to transform adversarial interactions into collaborative problem-solving efforts by managing the perception and reality of control.

Voss Concept

Alignment with D2 (Stakeholder Engagement)

Creating the Illusion of Control

Stakeholders are highly motivated by the need for autonomy and control. Voss’s methods ensure engagement by making the counterpart feel they are driving the outcome. This is achieved by asking Calibrated Questions (starting with "How" or "What"), which subtly guide the stakeholder to expend their mental energy on your problems, thereby co-opting their intelligence for your benefit.

Identifying Behind-the-Table Players

Effective engagement requires understanding the entire negotiation space, especially the motivations of individuals "behind the table" or Level II players who can act as "deal killers". Voss mandates asking calibrated questions like "How on board are the people not on this call?" to uncover these hidden stakeholders, preventing agreements from being sabotaged later.

Leveraging Worldviews (Black Swans)

Stakeholder engagement is deepened by uncovering Black Swans, the unknown unknowns, including the stakeholder’s "religion" (their worldview, values, or moral framework). By framing communication within the stakeholder's deeply held beliefs, the negotiator shows respect and gains profound normative leverage, leading to results that resonate with the stakeholder's internal standards.

Alignment with D3: Incident Management

Competency D3, Incident Management, addresses the need for structured action during critical incidents. Voss’s work is based entirely on the FBI's evolution away from brute force toward simple psychological tactics necessary "to calm people down, establish rapport, gain trust, elicit the verbalization of needs, and persuade the other guy of our empathy" during crises.

Voss Concept

Alignment with D3 (Incident Management)

De-escalation of Conflict

Incident management relies on de-escalating tension. Voss’s primary tools, like Labeling negative emotions ("It seems like you're worried about X"), apply rational language to diffuse intense feelings, shifting activity away from the fear-generating amygdala. This moves the counterpart to a calm, logical state where problem-solving can occur.

Focus on Execution ("How" over "Yes")

In incidents where life or critical operations are at stake, verbal agreement is meaningless without execution. By using the calibrated question "How am I supposed to do that?", the incident manager gracefully pushes back against demands while forcing the other party to articulate the implementation process in their own words. This guarantees buy-in and makes the solution "their idea," which is crucial for successful follow-through during a crisis. The failure of the St. Martin Parish prison siege illustrates the danger of a lack of confirmed execution details.

Slowing the Pace of Crisis

Going too fast is a mistake all negotiators make, which can undermine trust and rapport. In critical incident response, intentionally slowing the process down helps to calm the situation ("After all, if someone is talking, they’re not shooting"). Voss’s techniques, especially the extensive use of silence, Mirrors, and open-ended questions—are designed to draw out the process, reducing impulsive, rash decisions often associated with crisis timelines.

The alignment is clear: Voss's crisis negotiation methods provide highly tactical, psychologically grounded tools for achieving the foundational requirements of effective Communication, Stakeholder Engagement, and Incident Management outlined in the INSSA Core Competencies. The goal shifts from merely surviving the interaction to using managed conflict as a mechanism for discovery and guaranteed execution.

Voss Negotiation Concepts and Other INSSA Competency Alignment

The negotiation concepts advanced by Chris Voss align significantly with several INSSA Core Competencies beyond Communication (C3), Stakeholder Engagement (D2), and Incident Management (D3), particularly those involving information gathering, risk mitigation, and successful organizational implementation.

Here are the areas of alignment found in the INSSA Competency Framework:

A. Risk Assessment (A2: Establishing Context, A3: Risk Identification, A4: Risk Analysis, A5: Risk Evaluation)

Voss's methodology is inherently a form of psychological investigation aimed at gathering the deepest available information, which is fundamental to accurate risk assessment.

  • Uncovering Black Swans for Risk Identification (A3): Black Swans—the unknown unknowns—are pieces of information that, if uncovered, fundamentally change the entire dynamic of the negotiation or the security environment. Unearthing these hidden constraints, hidden interests, or critical facts through persistent probing is essential for comprehensive risk identification and analysis (A3, A4). Voss’s experience shows that apparent irrationality (acting "crazy") is often a clue that the counterpart is either ill-informed, constrained, or complying with hidden interests, all of which represent high risks that must be accurately identified (A3) and analyzed (A4).
  • Establishing Context Through Calibrated Questions (A2): Effective risk assessment begins with establishing context (A2). Voss’s Calibrated Questions (using "What" and "How") are designed to get the other side to reveal their thinking and teach the negotiator about their concerns. By asking "What is the biggest challenge you face?" or "What are we up against here?", the security professional can gather contextual data required for identifying threats and vulnerabilities in the environment (A2, A3).
  • Testing Assumptions (A4): Voss stresses that negotiators should view assumptions as hypotheses to be tested rigorously during the interaction, rather than committing to them. This mindset aligns with the critical function of risk analysis (A4), which requires objective data to test and verify potential threat scenarios.

B. Principles, Policies and Protocols (B4: Compliance, Performance and Effectiveness, B5: Information Management)

Voss's emphasis on verifying commitment and deep understanding provides mechanisms for ensuring policy adherence and high-quality information management.

  • Normative Leverage and Compliance (B4): Normative leverage involves using the counterpart's own internal norms, rules, or standards ("their religion") to advance a position. In a professional or organizational context (B4), understanding a counterpart’s self-imposed standards of conduct allows the SRMP to frame agreements in a way that respects those norms, making genuine compliance (B4) more likely because the person is deferring to their own authority structure.
  • Information Validation (B5): The negotiation process is inherently an exercise in information management. Voss provides specific tools for testing the validity and depth of information provided by a counterpart (B5):
  • The Rule of Three: Demanding that a commitment or confirmation be repeated in three different ways (using labeling, summarizing, or calibrated questions) is a tactic to guarantee the affirmation is not a counterfeit "yes" or a lie, thereby ensuring the quality of managed information.
  • Pronoun Usage: Paying attention to whether a counterpart is using "I/me/my" versus "we/they/them" can indicate their relative importance in the decision chain and reveal whether the information being received is coming from the true authority (B5).

C. Leadership, Management and Implementation (C5: Employee Management, C6: Innovation and Change Management)

The psychological foundation of Voss’s work offers tools for managing personnel dynamics and driving organizational change.

  • Employee Management (C5): The core active listening techniques are designed to manage interpersonal relationships by validating emotions and creating safety. Labeling is specifically employed to identify and diffuse negative emotions, allowing for rational thought to return. Using the Positive/Playful Voice and Mirroring helps build rapport even in difficult conversations, improving employee engagement and conflict resolution (C5). The practice of recognizing constraints (Mistake #2: They are constrained) helps managers address operational difficulties or internal limitations affecting an employee’s performance (C5).
  • Innovation and Change Management (C6): Voss's approach focuses on achieving deep, executable change rather than temporary compliance.
  • Overcoming Resistance to Change: Techniques like the Accusation Audit address negative dynamics and fears upfront (e.g., "You may feel like we have treated you unfairly"), clearing the psychological barriers that impede change and innovation (C6).
  • The Power of "No": Voss argues that resistance ("No") is often the starting point for negotiation because it signals where a person needs safety and control. By addressing this resistance directly, the manager enables the counterpart to own the outcome, which is crucial for successful implementation of new policies or innovative practices (C6).

D. Critical Incident Response (D1: Incident Identification and Response, D4: Incident Follow-Up, D5: Incident Review)

Beyond the hands-on management (D3), Voss’s methods directly inform the preparation and conclusion phases of crisis management.

  • Initial Response Tactics (D1): In the immediate aftermath of an incident, the primary goal is often to stabilize the situation. Voss emphasizes the importance of slowing down the conversation. This prevents impulsive decisions and allows time for discovery ("if someone is talking, they’re not shooting") (D1). The initial use of the Late-Night FM DJ Voice conveys authority and calmness, immediately helping to de-escalate the initial response phase (D1).
  • Implementation and Review (D4, D5): Voss stresses that the deal itself is useless without guaranteed execution. For D4 (Incident Follow-Up) and D5 (Incident Review), the focus shifts to ensuring accountability and improving future responses. The failure in the St. Martin Parish siege serves as a stark warning about poor implementation. A thorough incident review (D5) must examine why agreements failed, often leading back to the revelation of Black Swans (unimagined failures or hidden constraints) that were missed during the initial response and follow-up phases.

The alignment between Voss's psychological negotiation model and the INSSA competencies demonstrates that skills honed in high-stakes crisis environments offer directly applicable, tactical tools for essential security risk management functions, especially wherever human behavior and decision-making introduce variables that cannot be solved by logic alone. This is akin to realizing that the best map to navigating complex terrain isn't just topographical (the rational plan) but also cultural (understanding how people behave on the ground).
