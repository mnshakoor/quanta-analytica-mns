Strategic Plan

Integrating Partnership Principles into Security Risk Management

By M. Nuri Shakoor, SRMP-C | ARAC International Inc.

1.0 Introduction: The Strategic Imperative for Partnership-Centric Security

This strategic plan mandates our organization’s deliberate transformation from a traditional, compliance-based Security Risk Management (SRM) model to a more equitable, effective, and partnership-centric approach. This is not an incremental adjustment; it is a fundamental shift in our security culture, policy, and practice. Our evolution is guided by the principles articulated in the Global Interagency Security Forum (GISF) Partnerships and Security Risk Management: a joint action guide [1], with the explicit goal of achieving robust alignment with Competency Area B (Principles, Policies and Protocols) of the International NGO Safety and Security Association (INSSA) standards. This document provides senior management and security focal points with a clear roadmap for driving these systemic changes. It outlines a comprehensive approach embedded across four interdependent strategic pillars, designed to transform how we manage risk with our partners, moving decisively from risk transfer to a model of genuine risk sharing.

2.0 Foundational Principles: Shifting from Risk Transfer to Risk Sharing

Effective SRM in partnership settings cannot be built on compliance checklists and top-down assessments of partner capacity. It requires a foundation of trust, equity, and mutual respect [1]. We must adopt a new philosophy that changes the very nature of our security dialogue with partners:

  • Equitable Partnerships: We commit to valuing our partners' contextual knowledge and contributions as equal to our own. This principle ensures their security concerns are heard, understood, and addressed with the same priority as ours [1].
  • Joint Accountability: We will move decisively away from "top-down assessments" toward a model where duty of care is a shared, two-way obligation. Accountability will become a mutual mechanism [1].
  • Risk Sharing, Not Transfer: We strategically commit to openly discussing, analyzing, and jointly managing security risks. This principle prohibits the intentional or unintentional transfer of disproportionate risk to our local and national partners [1].
  • Contextualized and Co-Created Standards: We will develop and adapt SRM policies and procedures with our partners. This ensures they are appropriate for the local context, accessible, and operationally relevant [1].

3.0 Strategic Pillar 1: Re-engineering Policy and Standards (INSSA B1 Alignment)

3.1. Strategic Objective

To redesign and develop security policies and standards that are coherent, contextualized, rights-respecting, and explicitly integrate a partnership-based SRM approach as a core organizational mandate [1].

3.2. Key Strategic Actions

  1. Integrate a Partnership SRM Mandate into Core Policy: Revise the global security policy to include a dedicated "Partnership SRM" section, mandating comparison of partner risk thresholds.
  2. Institutionalize Joint Policy Reviews: Mandate the use of the GISF *Joint SRM Review Questionnaire (Tool 3)* as a standard checklist during all partnership inception phases.
  3. Embed Equity in Policy Language: Require the explicit incorporation of "equitable risk-sharing" and anti-bias language into the SRM policy, prohibiting unequal security standards.
  4. Mandate Policy Adaptation and Translation: Establish a formal requirement that all SRM policies and SOPs affecting partners must be translated and adapted with direct partner input.
4.0 Strategic Pillar 2: Defining Joint Accountability and Responsibility (INSSA B2 Alignment)

4.1. Strategic Objective

To establish and document clear, shared SRM roles, responsibilities, and lines of authority for every partnership, ensuring duty of care is treated as a joint obligation and accountability is mutual, transparent, and enforceable [1].

4.2. Key Strategic Actions

  1. Standardize SRM Governance in Partnership Agreements: Require every agreement to include a formal SRM Accountability Statement and a simple SRM Governance Diagram.
  2. Integrate Mutual Duty of Care Assessments: Revise all partner due-diligence tools to incorporate GISF's duty of care questions, ensuring *both* partners' obligations are documented.
  3. Establish a Joint Concern-Reporting Mechanism: Mandate the creation of a joint, confidential procedure for reporting and escalating SRM-related concerns.
  4. Embed SRM Responsibilities in Human Resources Systems: Direct HR to integrate SRM responsibilities identified in joint reviews into relevant job descriptions and performance management systems.
5.0 Strategic Pillar 3: Integrating SRM into Planning and Budgeting (INSSA B3 Alignment)

5.1. Strategic Objective

To systemically integrate joint SRM planning and costing into all phases of the project cycle, ensuring all identified security requirements are properly and equitably resourced for all partners involved in program delivery [1].

5.2. Key Strategic Actions

  1. Mandate Joint Risk Assessment and Costing: Establish the use of GISF's *Joint Security Risk Assessment and Management Plan (Tool 5)* and *SRM in Partnerships Budget Template (Tool 6)* as minimum requirements.
  2. Introduce SRM Budget Checkpoints in the Project Cycle: Update SOPs to include a mandatory SRM checkpoint at the proposal design stage to verify sufficient security budget lines.
  3. Formalize SRM Funding Discussions in Partnership Management: Make SRM funding a standing, required agenda item for all partnership inception and regular review meetings.
  4. Develop Staff Capacity in Risk-Based Budgeting: Initiate a targeted training program for all programme, grants, and finance staff on how to link security risk assessments directly to defensible SRM budgets.
6.0 Strategic Pillar 4: Institutionalizing Joint Compliance and Learning (INSSA B4 Alignment)

6.1. Strategic Objective

To implement a systematic, joint framework for monitoring SRM compliance, analyzing performance, and fostering a shared culture of learning and continuous improvement within and across partnerships [1].

6.2. Key Strategic Actions

  1. Establish a Joint SRM Review Calendar: Mandate the creation of a joint SRM compliance and review calendar for each long-term partnership, triggered by set timeframes or significant contextual changes.
  2. Develop Standardized Joint Monitoring Tools: Task the global security unit with creating and disseminating a simple SRM Compliance Checklist and a shared Post-Incident Learning Template.
  3. Formalize Compliance Responsibilities in Job Descriptions: Ensure that SRM compliance monitoring responsibilities are formally embedded into the job descriptions of all named SRM focal points.
  4. Integrate SRM Capacity into Performance Frameworks: Require all partnership monitoring frameworks to include specific, measurable performance indicators for SRM capacity-strengthening.

7.0 Conclusion: A Vision for Safer, More Equitable Partnerships

This strategic plan charts a course for a fundamental shift in our approach to security—from a unilateral, risk-averse function to a core enabler of effective and equitable partnership. It moves our practice from asking "are our partners compliant?" to "are we managing risk together effectively?".

The four pillars—Policy, Accountability, Planning, and Compliance—work in concert to create a holistic system that enhances our duty of care for all staff, respects the expertise of our partners, and strengthens the resilience and impact of our joint programming. By rigorously implementing this plan, we will not only fulfill our duty of care more effectively but also build the resilient, trust-based partnerships necessary to deliver greater humanitarian impact in the world's most challenging environments. This is how we become a leader in the sector.
