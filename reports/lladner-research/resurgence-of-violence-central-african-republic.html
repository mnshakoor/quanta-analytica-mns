Executive Summary

Violence resurged soon after the April 19 2025 N’Djamena Accord as 3R and UPC dynamics reappeared despite a formal dissolution dated July 10 2025. The June 28 2025 incident near Nzakoundou showed FACA unable to hold terrain, which signaled structural DDR failure tied to logistics, economics, and politics [1][2]. Capacity-commitment mismatches, weak extractive governance, and external actor complexity compounded risks [3][4][5][6][7].

  • DDR logistics shortfalls at cantonment sites drove recidivism and banditry risks [1][2].
  • Governance fragility and elastic ineffectiveness limited enforcement of the accord [2][3][7].
  • Arms embargo lift without oversight risked militarized patronage rather than institutional reform [2][3].
  • Identity-territory linkages and resource control kept violence economically viable [2][3][5].
1. Introduction

The CAR conflict is historically rooted in weak governance and fragmented authority. The N’Djamena Accord sought to demobilize 3R and UPC and reintegrate combatants into civilian life or FACA, but optimism faded quickly as violence re-emerged within weeks [1][2][3].

This report analyzes immediate post-accord relapse with focus on DDR failure, political-institutional weaknesses, humanitarian impacts, external actor roles, and structural incentives, closing with policy measures.

2. Failure of Disarmament and Reintegration (DDR)

2.1 Logistical Challenges

Sites such as Bokolobo, Maloum, Nzacko, and Yaloke reported shortages of food, water, and shelter [1][2]. MINUSCA presence was overstretched while FACA perimeter security proved unreliable in areas like Nzakoundou, illustrated by the June 28 event [1][2].

2.2 Economic Challenges

A promise to reintegrate 5,000 combatants lacked funding and scalable livelihoods plans. With a projected 2025 GDP at subsistence levels and donor pipelines strained, ex-combatants faced strong incentives to revert to armed activity, especially around Dawala, Thicka, and Sataigne [1][2].

2.3 Political Challenges

The accord assumed state capacity that did not exist. Identity-territory functions of 3R and UPC were not replaced with legitimate representation or revenue-sharing mechanisms, so rearmament remained a rational path [2].

2.4 Capacity-Commitment Mismatch

The state shoulders DDR burdens without the means to secure sites or finance reintegration, producing partial compliance and relapse [2]. Violence persists as an economic modality when reintegration benefits fail to materialize [2].

3. Political and Institutional Weaknesses

3.1 Governance Fragility

BTI 2024 classifies CAR as fragile with weak monopoly of force outside Bangui and porous borders that enable armed group mobility [3]. World Governance Indicators remain low across core pillars [2].

3.2 Elastic Ineffectiveness

Failures are absorbed without correction. Promised reintegration since 2017 lacked durable mechanisms, and power centralization did not produce operational coherence [2][7].

3.3 Extractive Governance Gaps

EITI 2024 shows only USD 2.6M in 2021 revenues, poor disclosure, and weak archives. Opacity undermines fiscal capacity and enables illicit resource capture [5].

3.4 Corruption and Transparency

IMF diagnostics highlight entrenched corruption and weak administrative capacity. Recommended legal, digital, and supervisory reforms show limited traction [4][7].

3.5 Monopoly of Force and Borders

State control is rare beyond the capital. FACA depends on external partners and struggles to hold terrain [3].

3.6 Electoral and Constitutional Issues

The 2023 constitutional shift expanded executive tenure and narrowed eligibility. Local elections slipped repeatedly, reducing checks and civic inclusion [7].

3.7 Repression of Opposition and Civil Society

Freedom House notes intimidation, arrests, and constraints on media and NGOs. Allegations of abuses implicated state and external security elements [7].

3.8 Media Suppression and Social Cleavages

Limited independent media and hate content inflame cleavages. Many Muslims remain displaced or confined to enclaves under persistent targeting [7].

4. Humanitarian and Civilian Impact

4.1 Displacement and Insecurity

Over half the population required assistance in early 2023. Hundreds of thousands remained refugees or IDPs, with renewed violence touching all 16 prefectures [2][7].

4.2 Basic Needs

Shortages at cantonment and community levels constrain food, water, shelter, and health access. Economic fragility limits state service delivery, increasing aid dependency [1][2].

4.3 Recidivism Risk

Ex-combatants without livelihoods or security are pulled back into armed activity or crime. Cantonment bottlenecks amplify this loop [2].

4.4 MINUSCA Role

MINUSCA’s civilian protection mandate faces mobility and logistics constraints. New flashpoints strain coverage while aid worker targeting continues [1][7].

5. Role of External Actors

5.1 MINUSCA

Tactical presence in flashpoints supports stabilization but remains overstretched without parallel FACA holding capacity [1].

5.2 Russia - Wagner Group

Wagner presence since 2018 supported territorial gains for the government and came with serious rights concerns and EU sanctions context [3][7].

5.3 African Union

AU endorsed dissolutions of 3R and UPC on July 10 2025, but rapid relapse showed limited enforcement leverage [1].

5.4 Neighboring Countries

Chad and Cameroon are essential for interdiction of supply lines. Rwanda and Angola engaged in Luanda Roadmap mediation [1][3].

5.5 Arms Embargo Lift

Lifting the embargo without strong oversight risks fueling patronage networks. Security sector reform and accountability need to track any materiel growth [2][3].

6. Structural Incentives for Violence

6.1 Violence as Economic Modality

Where reintegration fails, rearmament restores control over local economies and routes, turning violence into a sustaining enterprise [2].

6.2 Identity - Territory Linkages

3R protects pastoral routes while UPC seeks corridor control. Without new political venues that represent identity interests, rearmament remains rational [2].

6.3 Local Governance Gaps

Uneven statehood leaves power vacuums that militias fill with de facto rule [2][3].

6.4 Resource and Corridor Control

Mineral rents and corridor tolls finance operations. Weak extractive transparency enables illicit capture and conflict persistence [3][5].

7. Recommendations
7.1 Humanitarian surge and DDR

Stabilize cantonment logistics, secure sites with UN-authorized protection, deploy cash-for-work and training pathways, and track delivery with biometric vouchers [1][2].

7.2 Security plus reform

Joint FACA - MINUSCA operations to retake hotspots, reinforce discipline and rights safeguards, and coordinate cross-border interdiction with Chad and Cameroon [1][7].

7.3 Tiered reintegration

Prioritize high-risk zones with multi-year planning that blends livelihoods, reconciliation, and identity-linked governance venues [1][2].

7.4 Governance and transparency

Advance EITI-aligned disclosures, digitize PFM, strengthen legal oversight, and protect civil society and media for accountability [4][5][7].

7.5 International and regional alignment

Synchronize donor support with realistic state capacity, and leverage Luanda Roadmap partners for regional early warning and border control [1][3].
