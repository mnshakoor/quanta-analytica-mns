Executive Summary

This assessment presents a data-driven analysis of Mauritania's political and social landscape from 2006 to 2025. It reveals a state caught in a fragile equilibrium, where an external reputation for stability, crucial for partnerships with the EU and in Sahel counter-terrorism, masks deep-seated internal vulnerabilities. Explore the key risks, underlying data trends, and potential future scenarios to understand this complex dynamic.

The Core Finding: A Brittle Stability

Mauritania remains in a fragile equilibrium characterized by low democratic quality, entrenched grievances, and persistent human rights violations. The government's reputation for "stability" increasingly relies on coercive suppression rather than institutional resilience.

The data converge: weak governance (BTI, V-Dem), high fragility (FSI), and rising political violence (ACLED). The combination points to a brittle state more vulnerable than its external partners assume.

Future Scenarios (12-24 Months)

Based on the current trajectory and risk analysis, we project three plausible scenarios for Mauritania over the next one to two years. These scenarios are not predictions but structured possibilities designed to aid in strategic planning. They are weighted by likelihood, considering the deep-seated nature of institutional stagnation versus the potential for external pressures or internal escalations to alter the country's path.

1. Managed Stagnation

~60% Likelihood

This scenario reflects a continuation of the historical pattern. The government maintains its "stability" narrative with international partners, while internal dissent is managed through low-level coercion. Key indicators of democracy and fragility remain static.

ACLED Events: 5-15 annually

BTI/V-Dem: Static, low scores

FSI Score: Persistently high

2. Rights-Linked Conditionality

~25% Likelihood

Increased pressure from diaspora advocacy and UN mechanisms leads international donors to link aid more closely to human rights performance. This could force small but measurable improvements in policing, judicial processes, and activists' rights.

V-Dem Liberal Dem.: Slight gain

FSI Human Rights: Slight improvement

Key Trigger: Donor pressure

3. Crackdown Spiral

~15% Likelihood

A new lethal incident during a protest or in state detention triggers wider counter-mobilization. The state responds with a harsh crackdown, leading to a spike in political violence and prompting diaspora groups to campaign for sanctions.

ACLED Events: New peak (>15)

Diaspora: Escalates campaigns

Key Trigger: Lethal state violence

Context and Drivers of Instability

Understanding Mauritania's current risk profile requires acknowledging the deep historical and structural factors that shape its political landscape. These core drivers fuel the grievances visualized in the data and create the conditions for the scenarios outlined. From unresolved historical atrocities to systemic discrimination, these issues form the foundation of the country's fragility.

Historical Legacy: Unaddressed Atrocities

The 1989–1991 deportations, massacres, and arbitrary killings of Black Mauritanians remain a deep, unhealed wound. The passage of Amnesty Law 93-23 has ensured impunity for perpetrators, preventing transitional justice and embedding grievance at the heart of the nation's identity politics.

Structural Exclusion

Persistent ethnic discrimination is institutionalized through various state mechanisms. The 2011 biometric registration system has been widely criticized for systematically denying citizenship to Black Mauritanians. This, combined with ongoing land dispossession, fuels the high "Group Grievance" scores seen in the FSI data.

Shrinking Political Space

The government actively suppresses dissent. Opposition parties, civil society activists, and particularly anti-slavery groups like the Initiative for the Resurgence of the Abolitionist Movement (IRA), face routine repression, arbitrary detention, and police violence. This stifles internal channels for peaceful change.

Geopolitical Role & Socioeconomic Pressures

International partners, especially the EU and US, prioritize Mauritania's role in stability and counter-terrorism, often shielding the regime in Nouakchott from stronger human rights pressure. This external dynamic intersects with severe internal socioeconomic pressures like high unemployment, climate stress, and weak public services, compounding the population's vulnerability and discontent.

Data-Grounded Recommendations

The following recommendations are derived directly from the report's findings and are tailored for different stakeholders. They focus on leveraging data for more effective monitoring, advocacy, and risk mitigation, with the goal of shifting the international approach from one that enables brittle stability to one that promotes genuine, rights-based resilience.

For Analysts and Monitors

  • Develop Indicator Dashboards: Track BTI, V-Dem, FSI, and ACLED data quarterly, using specific thresholds (e.g., ACLED annual events >15) as triggers for escalation warnings.
  • Triangulate Claims: Systematically cross-validate claims from diaspora and activist groups with quantitative data spikes in V-Dem equality indicators and ACLED protest/repression events.

For Advocacy Groups

  • Bundle Evidence: Frame advocacy messages by combining powerful narratives (like the Kaédi killings) with hard data, such as the ACLED 2024 peak and V-Dem's negative equality scores.
  • Target Policy Levers: Focus campaigns on concrete, measurable demands like the repeal of Amnesty Law 93-23 and verifiable reforms to the biometric citizenship system.
  • Counter the Narrative: Actively use data showing repression-driven fragility to challenge the "stable partner" narrative often used by the government and its allies.

For Risk Mitigation & Donor Engagement

  • Protect Communities: Increase monitoring and protective presence in high-risk towns like Kaédi, which have proven to be flashpoints for state violence.
  • Provide Legal Defense: Prioritize resources for the legal defense of activists targeted for detention, as this is a primary tool of state repression.
  • Advocate for Conditionality: Engage with donor governments and institutions to tie development and security aid to measurable improvements in human rights indicators.
