Introduction: Your Partner in the AI-Powered Classroom

Let’s explore how Artificial Intelligence can serve as your new partner in the classroom. The goal here isn't to overhaul your curriculum but to introduce small, manageable shifts that leverage AI to enhance what you already do best.

Think of AI not as a replacement, but as a new kind of thought partner—a tool that, when guided by your expertise and inquiry, can help foster deeper critical thinking, boost student engagement, and cultivate the agency our students need. This module is designed to build your confidence and competence by providing practical strategies, ethical frameworks, and essential resources to help you integrate AI into your classroom responsibly and effectively.

1.0 Module 1: Foundational Concepts - Putting the Teacher in the Loop

Before we dive into classroom applications, it's essential to build a solid foundation. This module introduces the basic capabilities of AI and, more importantly, establishes a human-centric philosophy for its use.

Key Focus Points:

  • AI tools are augmenting, not replacing, the educator's role.
  • Understanding how Large Language Models (LLMs) work and their current limitations.
  • Establishing the critical need for "the teacher in the loop" to guide, verify, and contextualize AI outputs.

2.0 Module 2: Practical Classroom Integration

This module provides tangible, low-stakes methods for integrating AI tools into daily teaching practices and student learning activities.

2.1 Strategies for Educators (Teacher Agency)

Task Traditional Method AI-Assisted Method
Differentiation Manually adapting assignments for various levels. Use AI to instantly generate scaffolded versions of an existing prompt or create a rubric tailored to specific learning needs.
Brainstorming Starting a lesson plan from scratch. Use AI as an "Idea Generator" to develop multiple lesson plan outlines, starter prompts, or creative project themes.
Assessment Prep Writing multiple-choice questions. Use AI to generate a bank of questions based on a provided text, then review and refine for quality and complexity.

2.2 Strategies for Students (Student Agency)

  • The AI as a Tutor: Students can ask AI to explain complex concepts in simpler terms or in an analogy, personalizing their learning journey.
  • The AI as a Draft Partner: Students can use AI to outline an essay or summarize research articles, then focus their energy on critical analysis and personal voice.
  • The AI as a Critical Friend: Students can ask AI to find biases or weak points in their own arguments, fostering self-critique and argumentation skills.

3.0 Module 3: Ethical Frameworks and Responsible Use

This module focuses on the essential conversation around AI literacy—teaching students and staff how to interact with AI responsibly.

Three Pillars of AI Literacy

1. Transparency & Citation (The Honesty Principle)

Establish clear policies on when and how students must cite the use of AI. Teach students to treat AI outputs like any other source: always check the source.

2. Bias & Misinformation (The Scrutiny Principle)

Discuss how AI reflects biases. Introduce concepts like "hallucinations" (AI generating false information) and teach essential fact-checking skills.

3. Data Privacy & Security (The Protection Principle)

Stress the importance of not entering personally identifiable information (PII)/ into public AI models, aligning with school data policies.

"AI is a tool for inquiry, not an answer key."
